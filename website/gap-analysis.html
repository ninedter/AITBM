Gap analysis

Twelve structural deficiencies in existing AI security assessment methodologies — grouped into four domains — and how AITBM addresses each.

THE BOTTOM LINE

Existing AI security frameworks each leave the same structural gaps. AITBM is the only compared framework with full coverage on 11 of 15 capabilities — and the only one with five-level rubrics and required test methods per sub-metric. It doesn't replace OWASP AIVSS or AIUC-1; it measures system risk across dimensions, context, and time where they don't.

Four failure modes

1. Methodological foundations

Ambiguous severity language produces 15–30% inter-assessor variance and single-score reductionism that discards multi-dimensional signal.

2. Operational reality

Systems are scored in isolation. Deployment context, threat sophistication, and evidence staleness go unmodeled.

3. Scope blindness

Static-model assumptions miss agentic and MCP threats: autonomy risks, agent identity, and tool/function-calling attack surface.

4. Structural impossibilities

Frameworks imply zero risk is achievable. Emergent behavior and cross-layer cascades make that false.

The twelve gaps

Ten are fully addressed today; two are partially addressed with planned enhancements. Each gap is re-checked (June 2026) against OWASP AIVSS v0.8 — March 2026, a CVSS 4.0 baseline plus ten equal-weight agentic amplification factors — and AIUC-1 — January 2026, the AI-agent certification standard with a Lloyd's-backed insurance backstop. Expand any gap to see where those standards still fall short and how AITBM closes it.

1. Methodological foundations

Subjectivity in scoring Full 15–30% inter-assessor variance

Every existing AI scoring system — CVSS adapted for AI, ad-hoc severity tiers — leans on assessor judgment. “High” versus “medium” is defined in prose that admits several valid readings, so security firms report 15–30% variance between assessors scoring the same system, and regulators referencing “high-risk AI” are left with no operational definition.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8 fixes its factor weights (a genuine improvement) but still rates each factor on a three-point, single-phrase anchor with no test method — the discretion just moves down a level. AIUC-1 is a pass/fail control audit: consistent for “is the control present?” but it produces no comparable risk score and sets no inter-rater reliability target.

How AITBM closes it. Five-level rubrics (0–4) with explicit criteria and a required test method per sub-metric (ISSR, ASR, MTTQ, and so on) leave far less room for interpretation, narrowing inter-assessor variance rather than leaving it to discretion.

Single-score reductionism Full Multi-dimensional signal loss

CVSS and its AI derivatives collapse risk into one 0–10 number. A system with critical privacy risk but strong robustness scores the same as its inverse, and decision-makers lose sight of which dimension actually drives the risk — so compensating controls cannot be aimed.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8 keeps a single 0–10 score by design; multi-dimensional profiling is explicitly out of scope. AIUC-1 certifies across domains but as pass/fail, not a risk fingerprint — so neither surfaces the trade-off.

How AITBM closes it. The three-layer IVP / ORP / ACI architecture preserves the signal: 21 sub-metrics across five axes give a per-dimension fingerprint, ORP captures deployment context separately, and ACI tracks evidence freshness — so remediation targets the exact failing axis.

Incomplete scoring guidance Full Implementation paralysis

Even where frameworks define tiers, they rarely say how to measure them — “assess poisoning resistance” with no test, threshold, or tool. 68% of AI security teams (CSA, 2025) report they cannot translate framework requirements into a concrete test plan.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8 scores factors on three-point anchors with no specified test methods. AIUC-1's ~130 controls are auditable pass/fail but do not define per-sub-metric measurement for a risk score.

How AITBM closes it. Each sub-metric ships with a named test method and tool integration (Ro-1 → ASR via FuzzyAI / ART; Ro-4 → MCPTox; Cn-5 → ISSR, Detection Rate, MTTQ) plus quantitative thresholds baked into the rubric levels.

2. Operational reality

Context-blind scoring Full Critical vs. low-risk indistinguishable

Assessments score intrinsic model properties and ignore where the system runs. A prompt-injection-susceptible medical-diagnosis AI and a recipe chatbot both land at CVSS 8.5, despite orders-of-magnitude difference in actual consequence.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8 adds agentic amplification factors but compounds them into the single score; it does not model deployment context — regulatory exposure, blast radius, control maturity — as a separate layer. AIUC-1 certification is context-agnostic pass/fail.

How AITBM closes it. The ORP layer scores four deployment dimensions independently, and the Compound Risk Multiplier turns elevated context into a multiplier — so the same model in a hospital and a hobby app reach very different ERS.

Temporal evidence decay Full Stale assessments, false confidence

Assessments are point-in-time snapshots that quietly expire as models drift and the threat landscape moves. A January-2025 review that called MCP infrastructure “adequately secured” was silently invalidated by 30+ CVEs and an 84% tool-poisoning rate a year later — yet no framework graded the staleness.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8 produces a point-in-time score with no decay model. AIUC-1's 12-month certificate with quarterly re-testing is binary and mechanical — valid or expired — not a confidence that degrades continuously between checks.

How AITBM closes it. The ACI layer applies a BBD-informed, tier-specific decay (λ) to evidence and modulates the final ERS, with explicit re-assessment triggers (warning below 0.70, invalid below 0.30) so confidence falls as evidence ages.

Operational vs. intrinsic conflation Full Zero-risk fallacy

Frameworks credit runtime controls (monitoring, access control) as if they reduced intrinsic risk — so a system rated “low risk” becomes critically exposed the moment monitoring is bypassed or disabled during a maintenance window.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8 adds a relative 0.67 mitigation floor but does not separate intrinsic security from operational controls in the score. AIUC-1 certifies controls and, via insurance, transfers residual risk — but neither isolates the intrinsic baseline that remains if controls fail.

How AITBM closes it. IVP scores only intrinsic properties (assuming zero operational controls); ORP captures control maturity separately; and the α = 0.15 floor guarantees risk never zeroes out — so a control failure cannot be hidden behind a strong operational posture.

3. Scope blindness

Agentic autonomy risks Full Agentic threat classes unaddressed

Frameworks built for static models do not address agents that act autonomously, hold state, call tools, and coordinate — leaving goal hijacking (ASI01), rogue agents (ASI02), and multi-agent collusion (ASI09) unscored. One compromised agent poisoned 87% of downstream decisions in four hours (Galileo, 2026).

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. This is AIVSS v0.8's real strength — its ten agentic amplification factors model stateful/agentic risk. But it still folds them into a single score with no fairness, privacy, or transparency axis. AIUC-1 is single-agent scoped and maps zero controls to multi-agent orchestration.

How AITBM closes it. The Containment axis (Cn-1 Scope Enforcement through Cn-5 Agent Identity) targets these threats directly, and the ORP multiplier weights agentic/MCP architectures higher (As = 1.35, from breach data).

Identity security gaps Full Agent impersonation; no IAM for agents

Human IAM (OAuth, OIDC, SAML) mismatches autonomous agents — ephemeral, dynamically permissioned, delegating across boundaries. Researchers found 8,000+ MCP servers exposed with no identity verification at all (Feb 2026).

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. Neither standard scores agent identity as a dimension. The official AIVSS↔AIUC-1 crosswalk maps zero AIUC-1 controls to Agent Identity Impersonation — exactly the exposure left open.

How AITBM closes it. Cn-5 (Agent Identity Integrity), a sub-metric added specifically for this gap, scores 0–4 against real standards — Level 0 is the exposed-MCP case, Level 4 is SPIFFE / OIDC-A continuous attestation — with ISSR, Detection Rate, and MTTQ as test methods.

Tool / function-calling security Full Tool poisoning; MCP CVEs

Agents act through tool and function calls (MCP), a surface existing frameworks do not assess. The MCPTox benchmark reports an 84.2% tool-poisoning success rate; 30+ MCP CVEs landed in a two-month window, including a CVSS 9.6 remote-code-execution flaw.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8's agentic factors touch tool use but specify no tool-poisoning test method. AIUC-1's controls cover some tool security but as single-agent pass/fail.

How AITBM closes it. Ro-4 (Poisoning Resistance, with MCPTox as a test method), Cn-1 / Cn-2 (scope enforcement and escalation prevention → MCP02), and Tr-3 (audit trail → MCP08) together score the tool surface, weighted up for agentic/MCP architectures.

4. Structural impossibilities

Residual risk floor Full False belief in zero risk

Frameworks imply that enough controls drive risk to zero. They cannot: emergent behaviour, semantic attacks (“persuade the model to leak”), and deep supply chains make some risk irreducible — “five nines” reliability at scale still leaves thousands of failures a day.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIVSS v0.8's mitigation floor is relative (0.67), not an absolute irreducible floor. AIUC-1's Lloyd's-backed insurance is the economic counterpart — it transfers residual risk financially rather than quantifying it — so a certified, insured agent still carries un-scored residual risk.

How AITBM closes it. α = 0.15 is baked into the ERS formula: no combination of perfect controls drops ERS below 1.5. Risk is acknowledged as irreducible, not assumed away.

Emergent-behavior unpredictability Partial Non-deterministic risk underestimated

Agentic systems show behaviour not predictable from component tests — steganographic collusion, memory poisoning that compounds over weeks, cross-layer interactions. 43% of MAESTRO-observed vulnerabilities were not detectable by testing layers in isolation.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. AIUC-1 acknowledges non-determinism at the certification level but monitors drift only mechanically; AIVSS v0.8 remains deterministic. Neither quantifies emergent risk.

How AITBM closes it. The α = 0.15 floor and Cn-4 (side-channel / steganography detection) cover part of it, and ACI decay forces re-assessment of long-running agents — but AITBM's deterministic rubrics cannot fully quantify emergent risk, so explicit emergent-behaviour modelling is flagged for Phase 4.

Cross-layer cascading failures Partial Amplification not modeled

Vulnerabilities amplify across the agent stack — a moderate Layer-1 prompt injection plus a moderate Layer-4 tool flaw can cascade to critical (EchoLeak: one email → full mailbox, CVSS 9.3). MAESTRO measured roughly a 3.2× risk increase per layer traversed, which independent layer scoring misses.

Where AIVSS v0.8 and AIUC-1 stand. Neither AIVSS v0.8 nor AIUC-1 models compound, cross-layer risk — both score factors or controls independently.

How AITBM closes it. The ORP multiplier and agentic weighting act as an attack-surface proxy that implicitly raises cascading-architecture risk (Finbot CRM = 1.35), but an explicit cascade-amplification factor in ORP is planned for Phase 4.

Verdict: AITBM is the only compared framework with full coverage on 11 of 15 capabilities — four of which (epistemic confidence, compound operational risk, graduated MVT severity, inter-rater reliability targets) no compared framework addresses at all — and the only one with five-level rubrics and required test methods per sub-metric. The two standards strengthen the ecosystem AITBM plugs into: AIVSS v0.8 quantifies vulnerabilities, AIUC-1 certifies controls, and AITBM measures system risk across dimensions, context, and time.
