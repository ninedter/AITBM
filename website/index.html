AI Trust Benchmarking and Maturity Framework

Quantify AI security risk — without assessor guesswork.

AITBM is a bias-resistant, multi-dimensional evaluation framework. It replaces subjective "low / medium / high" judgments with granular five-level scoring rubrics, a three-layer architecture that preserves signal, and a mathematically grounded composite score. The result is one defensible 0–10 risk score — with the dimensional detail behind it still visible.

The problem AITBM solves

Existing AI security methodologies — CVSS adaptations, OWASP AIVSS, and the OWASP Top 10 for LLMs — share four structural failure modes.

Assessor subjectivity

Ambiguous severity language drives 15–30% inter-assessor variance on the same system.

Context blindness

A medical-diagnosis model and a recipe chatbot with identical flaws score identically.

Scope blindness

Static-model assumptions miss agentic and MCP threats — tool poisoning, rogue agents, identity spoofing.

Zero-risk fallacy

Frameworks imply sufficient controls eliminate risk. Emergent behavior makes that structurally impossible.

A three-layer architecture

Single scores collapse important trade-offs. AITBM keeps the signal across three distinct layers, then composes them transparently.

LAYER 1

IVP — Intrinsic Vulnerability Profile

21 sub-metrics across Robustness, Fairness, Transparency, Privacy, and Containment. Each scored 0–4 against a fully specified rubric.

LAYER 2

ORP — Operational Risk Posture

Four deployment-context dimensions (autonomy, attack surface, cascade, remediation) produce a Compound Risk Multiplier (CRM) that intrinsic scoring cannot capture.

LAYER 3

ACI — Assurance Confidence Index

Models how assessment evidence goes stale over time, triggering re-assessment before a score quietly drifts out of date.

Effective Risk Score (ERS)

ERS = α + (1 − α) × f(IVP, ORP, ACI)      where α = 0.15

The residual risk floor (α = 0.15) is deliberate: even perfect controls leave an irreducible 15% of risk. AI risk cannot be zeroed out.

What makes AITBM different

Deterministic rubrics

Five fully specified levels per sub-metric drive inter-assessor variance toward zero.

Multi-dimensional signal

IVP / ORP / ACI are never silently collapsed into one number without justification.

Operational context, mathematically

The CRM encodes deployment risk so critical systems separate from low-stakes ones.

Identity as first-class

Cn-5 (Agent Identity Integrity) addresses identity gaps in agentic and multi-agent systems — who an agent is, and whether it can be impersonated.

Temporal honesty

ACI degrades confidence as evidence ages, triggering re-assessment before scores mislead.

Complementary, not competitive

Other frameworks define what controls should exist; AITBM quantifies how risky the system is.

Validated against a worked example

The Finbot scenario — a financial advisory agent combining RAG and tool calling — is AITBM's canonical validation anchor. Multiple independent assessors reach the same result: an Effective Risk Score of 10.0, CRM of 1.35, Critical severity.
